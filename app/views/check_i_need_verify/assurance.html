Choose the right level of assurance

You can choose how thorough you want your service's identity checks to be by picking a 'level of assurance'.

Which one you pick depends on how confident your service needs to be that a user is who they say they are.

To help make your choice, you should think about:

  • how many users will need to use your service
  • how well-known your service is - popular services are more likely to be threatened or attacked
  • how users will feel about using your service, for example will they be reluctant to use it or is there a chance they'd use it maliciously
  • if your service will check any additional information about your user, for example if there's a record of them in an existing database
  • if your service has been targeted by fraud or cyber crime in the past
  • what would happen if a user impersonated someone else to use your service

Level 1

You should use level 1 if:

  • users can't use your service to claim benefits or payments
  • your service will need to check other information about a user as well as their identity, such as their driving licence number

If your service also lets users update personal information about themselves then you might need level 2 instead.

Example The Driver and Vehicle Licensing Agency (DVLA) use level 1 in their view driving licence information service. This is because users can only get information from this service. They canâ€™t use it to apply for anything more valuable or change their details.

It takes users an average of 5 minutes to prove their identity with level 1. They only need to do this once.

They'll be asked a series of questions that only they know the answer to. They usually won't need to provide any additional documents.

Level 2

You should use level 2 if:

  • your service gives users benefits, money or anything else valuable
  • you need to able to take users to court if they fraudulently use your service

Example HM Revenue and Customs (HMRC) use level 2 in their file a Self Assessment tax return service. This is because a user could be fined if someone else pretends to be them and uses this service to give false information to HMRC.

It takes users an average of 15 minutes to prove their identity. They only need to do this once.

They'll usually need at least one document (for example a driving licence or passport) to prove their identity. They might also need financial documents, such as a bank or credit card statement.
